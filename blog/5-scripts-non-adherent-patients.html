5 Scripts for Non-Adherent Patients

📅 May 8, 2026 · 9 min read · Physical Therapy Communication

You've prescribed the perfect home exercise program. You've demonstrated each movement. The patient nodded along. Then they come back next week — and nothing changed.

Non-adherence is the single most common frustration PTs express in online forums. Research shows that 30-65% of patients do not complete their prescribed home exercises, depending on the condition and complexity.

📚 Source: Adherence literature synthesis. Jack et al. (2010) reported 30-65% non-adherence rates across rehabilitation settings.

But here's what works: curiosity, not judgment. Shame shuts patients down. Curiosity opens them up. The following scripts are research-backed and field-tested by PTs.

Script 1: The Gentle Opening

"That happens. Was there something specific that got in the way this week?"

Why it works: It assumes the patient had a genuine barrier, not a character flaw. It invites explanation instead of defense.

Script 2: The Collaborative Adjuster

"Let's look at the exercise plan together. If it's too much or too hard to fit in, I want to know. We can adjust."

Why it works: It positions you as an ally, not an authority. It gives the patient permission to be honest.

Script 3: The Honesty Appreciator

"The fact that you're telling me honestly means a lot. Let's figure out a version you can actually do. That's on me, not you."

Why it works: It removes shame entirely. When patients feel safe admitting non-adherence, you can actually solve the problem.

📚 Source: Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci) shows that autonomy-supportive communication improves intrinsic motivation and adherence.

Script 4: The Barrier Investigator

"What's getting in the way? Pain? Time? Forgetting? Something else? I've heard it all — no judgment."

Why it works: It narrows down the specific barrier so you can solve the real problem, not the symptom.

Script 5: The Smallest Step

"Let's start smaller. What's the tiniest version of this exercise that would still count? Even one rep counts."

Why it works: Research on habit formation shows that starting extremely small overcomes the initiation barrier. Once started, continuation becomes easier.

The Research Behind the Approach

According to Moelands et al. (2024) in Musculoskeletal Science and Practice, higher prescribed frequency and dosage of home exercises significantly lowers patient adherence. The sweet spot appears to be 2-4 exercises, 3-5 days per week.

📚 Moelands, J., et al. (2024). Higher prescribed frequency and dosage of home exercises lowers patient adherence. Musculoskeletal Science and Practice.

Additionally, Kearney et al. (2024) found that in-person supervised exercise had 4.2x higher odds of adherence than app-based exercise alone. Your presence matters — but how you communicate matters just as much.

📚 Kearney, R., et al. (2024). In-person supervised exercise vs. app-based exercise: An RCT on adherence. Rheumatology International.

Key Takeaways for PTs

  • Don't start with "Why didn't you do it?" → That triggers defensiveness
  • Do start with "What got in the way?" → That triggers problem-solving
  • Simplify the HEP → 2-4 exercises max
  • Build self-efficacy → Ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can do this?" If less than 7, solve that why
